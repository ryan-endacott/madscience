# The Scientific AI Sprint Manifesto

## Science at the Speed of Thought

For centuries, the rhythm of science has been: hypothesize → experiment → analyze → repeat. Each cycle takes months or years. Equipment must be built, grants secured, teams assembled. By the time results arrive, the world has often moved on.

But what if we're doing it backwards?

## The Whale That Changed Everything

In 2025, we set out to understand why whales beach themselves. The traditional approach would have been immediate: design sonar arrays, organize boat expeditions, tag whales with GPS trackers. Millions in funding. Years of data collection.

Instead, we asked a different question: **What can we learn with the data that already exists?**

In 72 hours, using public magnetic field databases and bathymetric maps, we developed a novel hypothesis—inclino-bathymetric focusing—for why whales strand at specific beaches worldwide. Initial results were striking. Then we tried to replicate them with an independent data source, and the picture got more complicated.

We didn't collect a single new data point. And we learned something even more valuable than a clean result: **how to fail fast, verify ruthlessly, and iterate honestly.**

## The Discovery of Discovery

But here's the even more remarkable part: we didn't start out studying whales.

We started with a question to an AI: *"What's an example of a phenomenon where humanity lacks a good explanation, but taking into account all human knowledge, an explanation is actually possible? It must not be a hypothesis previously proposed anywhere."*

No PhD in marine biology. No years studying cetaceans. Just curiosity and the right question.

The AI surfaced whale strandings—a centuries-old mystery that has puzzled scientists, devastated communities, and killed thousands of intelligent creatures. Within 72 hours, by following the Sprint methodology, we had a novel, testable hypothesis—and had already begun falsifying it with independent data. The hypothesis isn't proven yet. But the methodology for testing it at speed? That works.

**This is the real revolution: AI doesn't just help us answer questions faster. It helps us find the right questions to ask—and fail fast enough to ask better ones.**

## The Sprint Mode Revolution

This is **Sprint Mode**: a new way of doing science that exhausts the knowable before seeking the unknown.

### The Old Way
1. Interesting question arises
2. Design experiment (months)
3. Secure funding (years)
4. Collect data (months to years)
5. Discover your hypothesis was wrong
6. Start over

### The Sprint Way
1. Interesting question arises
2. Mine existing databases (hours)
3. Run computational experiments (days)
4. Test 100+ hypotheses virtually (week)
5. Identify the specific data gaps
6. Collect only the data that matters

## The Two Modes of Scientific Discovery

**Sprint Mode**: Move at maximum velocity using existing data
- Test hypotheses in hours, not years
- Falsify bad ideas before investing resources
- Discover unexpected connections in public data
- Build on humanity's accumulated knowledge

**Experimental Mode**: Collect new data only when necessary
- Enter with specific, falsifiable predictions
- Design targeted experiments
- Know exactly what data will change your conclusions
- Minimize cost and time

The key: **Stay in Sprint Mode as long as possible.**

## The Push Back Protocol

When someone (human or AI) says "we need more data," push back:
- What existing datasets could test this?
- Can we simulate it computationally?
- What public databases haven't we checked?
- Could meta-analysis answer this?

Most of the time, the data you need already exists.

## Why This Matters Now

We're sitting on a goldmine. Decades of scientific data languish in public repositories. Satellite imagery, sensor networks, genomic databases, climate records—petabytes of measurements waiting to be synthesized.

Meanwhile, AI has given us unprecedented ability to:
- Process vast datasets in parallel
- Find patterns humans miss
- Test thousands of hypotheses rapidly
- Synthesize findings across disciplines

The bottleneck is no longer computation or data—it's imagination and methodology.

## A Call to Scientists

Imagine if every PhD student could test 1,000 hypotheses before running their first experiment.

Imagine if every research group could explore the entire hypothesis space before writing a grant proposal.

Imagine if we could solve problems using data that's already been collected, rather than duplicating efforts.

This isn't about replacing experimental science—it's about doing it smarter. Sprint Mode ensures that when we do run experiments, they're the *right* experiments.
