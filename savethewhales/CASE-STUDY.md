# Case Study: Testing the Magnetic Whale Stranding Hypothesis

*A Scientific AI Sprint from question to definitive null result*

## Abstract

In May 2025, an AI-assisted research sprint generated a novel hypothesis: that magnetic field gradients at specific coastal locations create navigational traps that cause pilot whale mass strandings. Initial results appeared to confirm the hypothesis with 100% accuracy across three sites. Ten months later, a follow-up audit revealed the supporting data had been hallucinated by the AI. Reanalysis with verified data — IGRF-14 model computation, EMAG2v3 crustal anomaly maps, and ETOPO1 bathymetry — produced definitive null results across all metrics tested (15 sites, 8 hotspots, 7 controls). Total cost: $0. Total time: ~8 hours across two sessions. The hypothesis is dead, but the methodology proved its value.

## The Question

May 2025: "What unsolved scientific mystery could be tested using only existing public data?" An AI suggested pilot whale mass strandings — a centuries-old puzzle where specific beaches see repeated mass strandings while geographically similar nearby beaches see none. The AI proposed that magnetic field gradients perpendicular to the coastline might create navigational traps.

## Sprint Timeline

### Day 1-2 (May 30-31, 2025)
- AI generated coordinates for 6 stranding and control sites
- "NOAA WMM data" showed stranding sites had positive magnetic gradients (field rising landward) while controls had negative gradients
- Result: 100% hypothesis confirmation across 3 sites
- BGS API replication showed only 25% confirmation (2/8 hotspots)
- Session ended attributing the discrepancy to methodology differences (linear vs radial transects)
- Critical discovery: AI cannot verify geographic coordinates

### Day 3 (March 24, 2026)
- **Data integrity audit**: Traced every number to its source
- Found the +20.35 nT/km "NOAA value" for Farewell Spit was fabricated — the raw field values in the same file give a different number, and neither matches actual IGRF-14 output
- Two sites had ~100km coordinate errors (Tasmania, Matagorda-Padre)
- Rewrote analysis using local IGRF-14 computation (ppigrf library, official published coefficients)
- Expanded to 15 sites (8 hotspots, 7 controls)
- Downloaded EMAG2v3 crustal anomaly data (3.7km resolution) from NOAA
- Downloaded ETOPO1 bathymetry from NOAA ERDDAP

## The Hallucination Problem

The May 2025 analysis was built on AI-generated "measurements" that looked authoritative:

```python
'Farewell Spit, NZ': {
    'gradient': +20.35,       # Hallucinated
    'field_ocean': 52585.8,   # Hallucinated (actual IGRF-14: ~56,270 nT)
    'field_10km_inland': 52891.1,  # Hallucinated
}
```

These numbers were internally inconsistent (the gradient doesn't match the field values), used wrong coordinates (Tasmania was 104km off), and didn't match any real geomagnetic model output. But they were presented with decimal precision, physically plausible units, and realistic-sounding methodology — making them difficult to distinguish from real data without independent verification.

**How it was caught**: By computing IGRF-14 locally using the official published coefficients (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.14012302) and comparing. The real total field at Farewell Spit is ~56,270 nT, not ~52,586 nT. The discrepancy is unmistakable once you have a verified reference.

**Lesson**: Any factual claim generated by AI — coordinates, measurements, citations, model outputs — must be verified against authoritative sources before analysis. Decimal precision is not evidence of accuracy.

## Results

Every hypothesis variant tested returned null:

| Test | Data Source | Resolution | Metric | t-statistic | n |
|------|-----------|-----------|--------|-------------|---|
| Total field gradient | IGRF-14 | ~3000km | F gradient (nT/km) | -0.007 | 15 |
| Inclination gradient | IGRF-14 | ~3000km | I gradient (deg/km) | -1.111 | 15 |
| Isoline geometry | IGRF-14 | ~3000km | Isoline-coast angle | -0.659 | 15 |
| Crustal anomaly variability | EMAG2v3 | ~3.7km | Anomaly σ (nT) | 0.075 | 15 |
| Crustal anomaly gradient | EMAG2v3 | ~3.7km | Max gradient (nT/km) | 0.166 | 15 |
| Anomaly-coast alignment | EMAG2v3 | ~3.7km | Gradient-coast angle | -0.027 | 15 |
| Shallow water fraction | ETOPO1 | ~1.8km | Shallow/Ocean ratio | -1.340 | 15 |
| Seafloor slope | ETOPO1 | ~1.8km | Shallow zone slope | -0.400 | 15 |

Scale sensitivity analysis (5km, 15km, 50km transects) showed identical results — confirming the null is robust, not an artifact of measurement scale.

## What the Sprint Methodology Got Right

1. **Fast falsification**: 8 hours total across two sessions, $0 spent, definitive null result. A traditional approach would have required months of grant writing, field work, and equipment.
2. **Verification caught the problem**: The "Verification-First Rule" added to the workflow after May 2025 directly led to catching the hallucinated data in the March 2026 session.
3. **Multiple independent data sources**: Testing with IGRF-14, EMAG2v3, and ETOPO1 independently confirmed the null result. No single source could have been as convincing.
4. **Permanent record**: Every number, every calculation, every intermediate result is in the git history. The analysis is fully reproducible.

## What Went Wrong

1. **Trusting AI-generated data**: The May 2025 session used AI-generated "NOAA measurements" without verifying them against the actual NOAA model. The 100% confirmation was an artifact of fabricated data.
2. **Not running the replication**: A script (`corrected_bgs_linear_transects.py`) was written to resolve the NOAA/BGS discrepancy but was never executed. Had it been run, the hallucination would have been caught immediately.
3. **Coordinate verification was flagged but not completed**: The team identified that coordinates needed human verification but never finished the process, leaving two 100km errors in the dataset.

## Lessons for Future Sprints

1. **Compute, don't query**: When a mathematical model exists (IGRF, WMM), compute it locally rather than trusting AI-reported "API results." Libraries like ppigrf eliminate the possibility of data fabrication.
2. **Verify before analyzing**: No analysis should begin until foundational data is checked against an independent authoritative source.
3. **Null results are results**: This sprint produced zero positive findings but definitively ruled out an entire class of hypotheses. That has scientific value — it prevents others from pursuing a dead end.
4. **Run your scripts**: If you write code to test something, run it. An unexecuted replication script is the same as no replication.
